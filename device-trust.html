CyberCrowd Device Trust

CyberCrowd Device Trust defines how phones, cameras, monitors, kiosks, control surfaces, paired devices, and live system inputs may be trusted before they are allowed to view, control, broadcast, route, archive, or participate in the platform.

Device Trust Purpose

CyberCrowd cannot treat every device as safe just because it connects. A trusted multi-surface live system needs a way to define which devices belong, which devices can control, which devices can view, and which devices should be restricted, reviewed, or rejected.

Possible Trusted Devices

  • Phone input devices
  • Camera devices
  • Monitor surfaces
  • Kiosk surfaces
  • Creator control devices
  • Client control devices
  • Venue devices
  • Viewer access devices
  • Magic Cursor control devices
  • Surface OS devices
  • Archive review devices
  • Secure hardware nodes

Trust Before Control

A device should not gain control just because it appears on the network, pairs to a surface, scans a code, or opens a CyberCrowd window. Device Trust helps define whether the device has permission to control, view, broadcast, route, upgrade, or archive.

Trust Signals

  • Known device identity
  • Approved pairing state
  • Access pass status
  • Operator or creator permission
  • Surface role assignment
  • Secure hardware status
  • Tamper awareness signals
  • Session or event authorization
  • Revocation or recovery status
  • Archive or review flags

Device Rejection and Review

If a device appears out of place, fails trust checks, shows tamper signals, conflicts with an access rule, or attempts control without permission, CyberCrowd may reject, restrict, quarantine, or mark that device for review.

Connection to Surface Pairing

Surface Pairing connects a device to a surface. Device Trust decides whether that paired device should be allowed to do anything meaningful inside the CyberCrowd system.

Connection to WDIG

WDIG asks where the device belongs. Device Trust helps decide whether the device belongs at all, and whether it should be routed to control, viewing, archive review, restriction, or investigation.

Connection to Magic Cursor

Magic Cursor should only move through trusted control paths. Device Trust helps protect Magic Cursor from being used by untrusted, misrouted, or unauthorized devices.

Connection to Access Passes

Access Passes may define who or what has permission. Device Trust helps enforce whether a device, host, kiosk, controller, or surface is allowed to use that access.

Archive and Proof

Device trust events may become proof objects when they affect live control, access, security, client investigation, platform integrity, or governance. CyberCrowd may preserve important trust events in the Platform Archive.

Connection to the CyberCrowd System

Device Trust may connect to Surface Pairing, Surface Roles, Surface OS, Secure Surface Hardware, WDIG Routing, Magic Cursor, Access Passes, Live Control Room, Cam Packages, Viewer Upgrades, Creator Control, Client Investigation, Platform Archive, Atomic Kricket, and Reten Records.
