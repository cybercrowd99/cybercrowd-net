## CyberCourt Statement

CyberCourt is a CyberCrowd public discourse, survey, service-call, debate, policy-review, and platform-clarity concept.

CyberCourt is not a court of law.

CyberCourt is intended as a public realm for structured surveys, paid-member review, follow-up questions, public sentiment, repair paths, educational debate, market intelligence, policy feedback, and platform maintenance signals.

CyberCourt may later support public dockets, member survey privileges, paid debate rooms, service-call requests, educational mock-case review, and adWorm-sponsored participation pools.

CyberCourt does not replace CyberCrowd operational authority.

Core principles include:

- CyberCourt gets the questions.
- CyberCrowd keeps the keys.
- adWorm funds the room, not the verdict.
- CyberCourt studies needs before CyberCrowd hardens controls.
- CyberCourt gathers signals, not weapons.
- If CyberCrowd cannot hear the wheel, CyberCrowd cannot maintain the system.
- A loud squeak gets inspected. It does not automatically get control.
- A service fee may open the docket. It does not buy the outcome.
- Public sentiment may create precedent signals until overturned by new facts, better policy, or later public sentiment.

## Monetary System Statement

CyberCrowd includes planned monetary-system concepts for access products, creator income, viewer participation, sponsor exposure, adWorm campaigns, CyberCourt review pools, Reten Records media, and future CRWD platform value systems.

This repository may describe monetary direction, but public foundation language does not create a banking system, wallet, security, investment product, payout system, or token launch.

Core monetary principles include:

- CyberCrowd money must be clear, staged, auditable, and tied to real access, real participation, real sponsorship, or real platform value.
- Sell clear access products safely before building complex money rails.
- Me, Them, and They.
- No fake participation.
- No pay-to-control.
- No hidden money rails.

## Guardian and Child-Safe Access Statement

CyberCrowd may later support guardian-controlled child access for approved media, family-safe participation, learning, music, accessibility tools, and child-safe platform lanes.

Child profiles should not be treated like normal viewer accounts.

CyberCrowd should protect child safety, restrict adult discovery, avoid child profiling, prevent direct targeting, and keep guardian control over purchases, access, and visibility.

Core principle:

Kids can enjoy approved lanes. Kids cannot be targeted, monetized, contacted, profiled, or pulled into adult discovery.

## Identity Step-Up Statement

CyberCrowd may allow lower-friction viewer access while requiring stronger verification for creators, owners, broadcasters, payout recipients, channel operators, advertisers, sponsor agents, bot agents, and room controllers.

Core principle:

Watching can be low-friction. Creating, selling, broadcasting, owning, advertising, receiving payouts, or controlling rooms requires step-up verification.

## Access Records and No Silent Deletion

CyberCrowd should not rely on silent deletion as a trust mechanism.

Access events, identity step-ups, bot declarations, advertiser requests, guardian approvals, creator permissions, media submissions, complaint actions, restriction events, monetary events, campaign approvals, and public docket actions should leave durable records.

Records may be hidden, ignored, archived, disabled, revoked, blocked, quarantined, redacted, placed under legal hold, or legally removed where required.

CyberCrowd should preserve accountability without unnecessarily exposing private, sensitive, financial, or child-identifying information.

Core principle:

CyberCrowd can hide access. CyberCrowd can revoke access. CyberCrowd can archive access. CyberCrowd can redact what the law requires. But CyberCrowd should not allow silent erasure of platform history.
