CyberCrowd Rules Manual

Rules of the Game

CyberCrowd works best when everybody can see the board, understand the pieces, and know what happens next. This manual explains the system in plain language: no hidden fine print, no mystery switches, and no rule that only appears after someone loses a turn.

1. Object of the Game

The object of CyberCrowd is to help creators, clients, users, and businesses move through access, content, review, correction, and trust without turning confusion into punishment.

Plain rule: CyberCrowd does not hide the ball. If a rule matters, the rule belongs on the table before the play begins.

CyberCode holds the deeper memory. This Rules Manual explains the game so regular people can understand what the system is doing and why.

2. Players

Users

People watching, joining, subscribing, testing, voting, asking, learning, or using CyberCrowd tools.

Creators

People or teams offering content, broadcasts, camera packages, products, services, or creative work.

Clients

Businesses, partners, venues, brands, or operators using CyberCrowd systems for access, discovery, or trust workflows.

Reviewers

People helping sort issues, check facts, identify drift, and keep review tied to the issue instead of the profile.

CyberHR / CLER

The learning and correction role. CLER maps the work before judging the worker.

CyberCrowd Governance

The role that protects rules, access, escalation, remedies, records, and platform stability.

3. Game Board

The CyberCrowd board is not one square. It is a connected system of spaces where different kinds of play happen.

CyberCourt

The place where the question is framed clearly. CyberCourt controls the question, not the person.

Docket Forum

The entry table where issues can be placed, tracked, clarified, and routed.

Chopping Block

A public review table for visibility, pressure testing, and learning. It does not become the judge.

CyberCode

The deep rulebook and memory layer that records rules, corrections, lessons, and system history.

Business Sandbox

A practice and testing space where businesses, creators, and operators can learn before scaling mistakes.

Water Decrees

Plain statements that define flow, access, obligations, correction, and boundaries.

4. Game Pieces

Game pieces are the things that move through the board.

  • Issue: the question or problem being reviewed.
  • Complaint: a claim that something went wrong or needs review.
  • Docket Entry: the record that places the issue on the table.
  • Notice: a clear message that tells a player what is happening and why.
  • Grace Period: time to fix, answer, pay, clarify, or correct before stronger action.
  • Default: a temporary status when a duty is missed. Default is not forever.
  • Appeal or Recourse: a path to ask for review, correction, or explanation.
  • Recusal: stepping away from a decision when neutrality is at risk.
  • Open Seat: a temporary status when a decision-maker cannot safely participate in a vote.

5. Setup

Before play begins, CyberCrowd needs the board set cleanly.

  1. Name the issue clearly.
  2. Identify which players are involved.
  3. Separate the issue from the person’s profile.
  4. Check the rule that applies.
  5. Give notice when notice is required.
  6. Offer correction before escalation when correction is possible.
  7. Record the outcome so the same confusion does not become a hidden pattern.

Issue, not profile: Accent, origin, disability, class, education, appearance, religion, region, or learning style must not become the case.

6. Turn Order

CyberCrowd should move in order instead of jumping straight to punishment.

  1. Ask: What is the issue?
  2. Define: What rule, duty, or expectation applies?
  3. Notice: Has the player been told what is wrong?
  4. Answer: Has the player had a chance to respond?
  5. Correct: Can training, payment, access repair, role clarity, or better instruction fix it?
  6. Review: Is there drift, bias, hidden influence, or missing context?
  7. Escalate: If correction fails or risk is serious, stronger action may be used.
  8. Record: CyberCode remembers the lesson.

7. Chopping Block / Public Review Table

The Chopping Block is not a mob button. It is a public review table. It can reveal confusion, pressure, visibility, market fear, policy weakness, product misunderstanding, or unmet need.

Plain rule: Public sentiment can clarify the heat around an issue, but it cannot replace the rulebook.

CyberCrowd may learn from public friction without manufacturing enemies for engagement. A public attack is not always a wound. Sometimes it is market proof.

8. Sequester Event

A Sequester Event is a protected review moment. It lets a player separate identity, pressure, noise, or public heat from the issue being reviewed.

  • A player may be invited into Sequester when the issue needs cleaner review.
  • The player may be offered an identity choice when public exposure is not required.
  • Sequester does not erase accountability. It helps keep the review fair.
  • The goal is to protect the issue from becoming a profile attack.

9. Scoring & Signals

CyberCrowd scoring is not just money. Trust, clarity, correction, safety, and public understanding all matter.

Green Signals

Clear rule, fair notice, correction accepted, access repaired, payment resolved, trust improved.

Yellow Signals

Confusion, drift risk, unclear instruction, payment delay, public friction, missing context, review needed.

Red Signals

Hidden influence, deception, unsafe access, repeated default, retaliation, profile attack, concealed conflict.

10. Penalties and Remedies

Penalties should match the issue. CyberCrowd should not use invisible punishment.

  • Correction: training, clarification, repair, role change, or better instruction.
  • Restriction: temporary access limits when risk requires it.
  • Default: temporary status for missed payment, missed duty, or missed answer.
  • Open Seat: temporary decision pause when hidden influence or conflict threatens a vote.
  • Removal: stronger remedy when correction fails or serious harm requires it.
  • Record: CyberCode records the reason so action does not become hidden fine print.

No invisible punishment: If CyberCrowd restricts, defaults, removes, or limits a player, the reason must be tied to a visible rule or record.

11. Special Cards

Special cards are tools that can change the next move when fairness, learning, or safety requires it.

Grace Card

Allows time to fix a missed duty before stronger action.

Recusal Card

Removes a conflicted player from a decision before the decision is damaged.

Correction Card

Sends an issue to learning, role clarity, instruction, or system repair.

Sequester Card

Protects review from noise, identity pressure, or public pile-on.

Public Signal Card

Uses public friction as information without letting the crowd become the rulebook.

Open Seat Card

Temporarily pauses a decision-maker when hidden influence or disclosure failure threatens the board.

12. Winning Condition

CyberCrowd wins when people can use the system, understand the rules, fix mistakes, trust the process, and see that correction is stronger than confusion.

Winning does not mean nobody ever makes a mistake. Winning means mistakes do not become secret patterns, hidden penalties, rigged outcomes, or profile attacks.

Winning rule: Trust compounds when the rule is visible, the issue is clear, correction is possible, and the record remembers the lesson.
