CyberCrowd Hidden Influence

CyberCrowd Hidden Influence protects the platform from undisclosed pressure, concealed conflicts, covert attempts to alter decisions, incomplete disclosure, and hidden control that may affect trusted decision-making.

Honest disclosure protects CyberCrowd. Hidden pressure, concealed conflict, and covert control do not get to steer the platform.

Hidden Influence Purpose

CyberCrowd must protect the glass castle from decisions shaped by pressure that is not disclosed. A vote, access decision, board decision, client decision, creator decision, platform choice, or media decision can be damaged when influence hides behind silence, omission, private pressure, or incomplete disclosure.

This review path does not exist to accuse first. It exists so CyberCrowd can notice, define, disclose, review, correct, and record hidden influence concerns before they damage trust.

Possible Hidden Influence Signals

  • Undisclosed pressure from outside parties
  • Concealed conflicts of interest
  • Hidden financial interests
  • Private threats or promises connected to a decision
  • Covert attempts to alter board or platform outcomes
  • Incomplete disclosure before major votes
  • Refusal to answer required disclosure questions
  • Misleading silence during review
  • Attempts to use public pressure as hidden authority

Rule-on-the-Table Standard

Hidden influence review must begin with a visible rule and a named issue. The person should not become the issue. The issue is the pressure, conflict, omission, concealed interest, refusal, or attempted control.

The question is not who CyberCrowd dislikes. The question is what influence was hidden and whether it affected the decision.

Disclosure Before Major Decisions

Before major decisions, CyberCrowd may require relevant participants to disclose schedules, contacts, conflicts, pressures, and conversation contents connected to the decision being reviewed.

Honest disclosure protects the participant and CyberCrowd. Deceit, evasion, refusal, misleading silence, or incomplete disclosure may trigger review because the platform cannot protect a decision it cannot see.

For-Cause Review

Hidden influence may trigger For-Cause Review. A review does not automatically mean guilt. It means CyberCrowd has identified a serious enough signal to require definition, evidence, disclosure, correction, and procedural handling.

Temporary Open-Seat Handling

If a hidden influence concern affects a major vote or board decision, CyberCrowd may consider whether the affected seat should be treated as temporarily open for that decision until review or remedy is complete.

Open-seat handling protects the decision. It is not a secret punishment, not a public shame label, and not a final decision by itself.

Decision Integrity Check

Before a decision affected by hidden influence moves forward, CyberCrowd should check whether the decision path is visible and clean.

  • The issue is named.
  • The rule is visible.
  • The disclosure question is clear.
  • The person is not being turned into the issue.
  • Recusal or open-seat handling has been considered.
  • Correction or clarification has been considered before escalation.
  • Public pressure is treated as signal, not authority.
  • The next action is recorded.
Before CyberCrowd acts, the decision path must be visible.

Possible Remedies

  • Disclosure correction
  • Vote delay or vote invalidation review
  • Recusal or open-seat handling
  • Access restriction review
  • Seat review
  • Termination-for-cause review
  • Equity or token remedy review
  • Clawback review
  • Legal remedy review
  • Archive preservation of evidence

No Invisible Punishment

Hidden influence review must not become an invisible punishment system. Restriction, vote handling, access review, seat review, or escalation must connect to a visible rule and a visible path.

Hidden influence cannot be fixed with another hidden process.

Public Pressure Boundary

Public sentiment may clarify, but it does not govern. Public reaction may help CyberCrowd see confusion, concern, urgency, or pressure, but it cannot replace board duty, evidence, authority, or platform obligation.

A rule that cannot survive public challenge was not ready to govern, but the public can show us where the rules are weak. Public challenge may reveal a gap. It does not become hidden authority.

Public pressure may reveal a concern. CyberCrowd remains responsible for the decision.

Archive and Proof

Hidden influence review should preserve relevant proof: disclosures, refusal records, meeting notes, communications, timing, access logs, decision history, vote concerns, and any evidence needed to define what happened.

Correction and Recipe Rule

A definition gives the rule a container. It does not freeze the recipe. If lived experience, public challenge, evidence, or failure shows the hidden influence rule needs work, CyberCrowd may review, repour, remix, improve, and record the change.

Connection to CyberCrowd

Hidden Influence may connect to Governance, Ethics & Conduct, Boardroom, Platform Archive, Public Discourse, Client Investigation, Access Passes, Creator Control, Live Control Room, WDIG Routing, Magic Cursor, CyberCode, the CyberCrowd Rules Manual, and future board procedure systems.

Hidden influence review protects CyberCrowd from concealed control while keeping the rule on the table.
